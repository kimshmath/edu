Is the Universe Round?

The Greeks answered this question for the Earth with two sticks and a shadow. Twenty-three centuries later, we still cannot answer it for the universe.

§ 1 — The Earth Is Round

Shadows, ships, and the curvature of the world

For most of human history, the shape of the Earth was unknown. Stand in a field and look around: the ground stretches flat to the horizon in every direction. The idea that you are standing on a giant ball would have seemed absurd.

Yet by 500 BC, Pythagoras had proposed exactly that — on purely aesthetic grounds. The sphere, he believed, was the most perfect shape, and the cosmos deserved perfection. Aristotle (384–322 BC) went further and offered physical evidence: ships vanish hull-first over the horizon; the Earth's shadow on the Moon during an eclipse is always circular; and travelers who go south see new constellations rise above the horizon. None of this makes sense on a flat Earth.

And then came the decisive experiment.

Eratosthenes' experiment (c. 240 BC)

In Syene (modern Aswan), Eratosthenes had learned that at noon on the summer solstice, the Sun shone directly into a deep well — perfectly vertical, no shadow at all. Yet 800 km north in Alexandria, at the same moment, a vertical stick cast a shadow at about 7.2° from vertical. One-fiftieth of a full circle.

On a flat Earth, the Sun's parallel rays would strike both sticks at the same angle. The only explanation for different shadow angles at the same moment: the ground between the two cities is curved.

Multiply the distance by 50 and you get approximately 250,000 stadia — within 2% of the modern value of 40,075 km. Two sticks, one shadow, and a single multiplication had measured the entire planet.

Centuries later, Columbus (relying on a smaller, incorrect estimate by Ptolemy) dared to sail west. Magellan's expedition (1519–1522) completed the first circumnavigation. The question was settled: the Earth is round.

But an incomparably deeper question was already forming.

§ 2 — But What About the Universe?

A question too large to walk around

We proved the Earth is round by sailing around it. But what about the universe? No one can fly around the universe. No one can step outside it. We are trapped inside — like ants on a balloon, trying to guess the balloon's shape by crawling.

And yet, locally, space gives nothing away. Just as the Earth looks flat from your garden — the horizon straight, parallel lines parallel, angles summing to 180° — space looks perfectly Euclidean in every direction we can measure. The curvature, if it exists, hides at scales far larger than we can see.

In 1854, a young German mathematician named Bernhard Riemann delivered a lecture that changed how humanity thinks about space. His insight: a space can be locally flat yet globally curved. It can be finite in extent yet have no boundary. The mathematical name for such a space is a manifold.

What is a manifold?

An \(n\)-dimensional manifold is a space that locally looks like \(\mathbb{R}^n\) — ordinary Euclidean space — but globally may have a completely different shape. The surface of the Earth is a 2-manifold (locally \(\mathbb{R}^2\)). The universe is a 3-manifold (locally \(\mathbb{R}^3\)).

No matter which surface you started with — sphere, torus, or Klein bottle — when you zoom in far enough, you see exactly the same thing: a flat grid. That is what it means to be a manifold.

At very high zoom, every surface is indistinguishable from a flat square — the coordinate chart, the local patch of \(\mathbb{R}^2\) that makes it a manifold. The deep question is not what the patches look like — they all look the same — but how they fit together. That is what determines the global shape.

Three visions of the cosmos

Riemann (1854) proposed that the universe might be a 3-sphere \(S^3\) — the three-dimensional analogue of the surface of a ball. Finite in volume, no boundary, positively curved. Walk far enough in any direction and you return to your starting point. Einstein adopted this model in 1917 for his first cosmological solution.

Clifford and Klein (1873–1890), inspired by the discovery of a flat torus living inside \(S^3\), proposed a rival: the 3-torus \(T^3\). Flat, finite, multiply connected — a cube whose opposite faces are glued. Walk through the north wall and you re-enter from the south.

And Seifert and Weber (1933) showed that a third option exists: a closed hyperbolic 3-manifold, built by identifying opposite faces of a dodecahedron with a \(3\pi/5\) twist. A universe of negative curvature, where parallel lines slowly diverge.

Three candidates for the shape of everything:

\(S^3\) (round) · \(T^3\) (flat) · \(\mathcal{SW}\) (hyperbolic)

The question — is the universe round? — was born. To make progress, we must first understand the two-dimensional case: surfaces.

§ 3 — Surfaces

The classification of surfaces

Before we can say anything about the shape of the universe — a 3-manifold — we should understand the world one dimension down. The classification of surfaces is the warm-up problem. And it is already one of the most beautiful theorems in mathematics.

The classification theorem for surfaces says that every closed, connected surface is determined by just two things: whether it is orientable, and a single integer.

If the surface is orientable — meaning it has a consistent notion of "inside" and "outside" — then it is a sphere with some number of handles attached. That number is the genus \(g\). The sphere has genus 0. The torus (a doughnut) has genus 1. A pretzel-shaped surface has genus 2. And so on, forever.

If the surface is non-orientable — like the Möbius band or the Klein bottle — then handles are replaced by crosscaps, and the classification works by the number \(k\) of crosscaps. One crosscap gives the projective plane \(\mathbb{R}P^2\). Two give the Klein bottle.

Classification of Closed Surfaces

Every closed connected surface is homeomorphic to exactly one of:

\(S^2,\; T^2,\; T^2 \# T^2,\; T^2 \# T^2 \# T^2,\; \ldots\)  (orientable, genus \(g = 0,1,2,3,\ldots\))

\(\mathbb{R}P^2,\; K,\; \ldots\)  (non-orientable, \(k = 1,2,\ldots\) crosscaps)

The Euler characteristic \(\chi = 2 - 2g\) (orientable) or \(\chi = 2 - k\) (non-orientable) is a complete invariant.

§ 4 — The Flat Torus

A finite world with no edges and no curvature

Take a square piece of paper. Glue the left edge to the right edge. Glue the top edge to the bottom. No stretching, no crumpling — just identification. The result is the flat torus \(T^2\): a surface that is flat everywhere, finite in area, and has no boundary at all.

What would it feel like to live inside this surface? You would be standing in a square room. Walk to the right wall and you emerge from the left. Walk up and you drop in from below. The room has no doors and no walls — yet it is finite. It is the simplest example of a closed flat manifold.

If the universe were a 3-torus — a cube with opposite faces identified — you could fly in a straight line and eventually return home. Looking ahead, you would see copies of yourself receding into the distance, like standing between two parallel mirrors. A finite cosmos, completely flat, with no edge and no center.

Straight lines on a torus

Walk in a straight line on the flat torus. What happens depends on the slope. If the slope is rational — say \(p/q\) — the line winds around and eventually closes into a loop. But if the slope is irrational — \(\sqrt{2}\), say, or \(\pi\) — the line never closes. It goes on forever, winding tighter and tighter, gradually filling the entire surface. A single straight line, on a finite surface, that is dense.

§ 5 — Orientability

When left and right switch

Hold your right hand up to a mirror. The reflection is a left hand. On an orientable surface — the sphere, the torus, any surface with handles — this can never happen by walking. No matter what path you take, your right hand stays your right hand.

But on a non-orientable surface, there exists a path that brings you home mirror-reversed. Left and right have silently swapped. The simplest example is the Möbius strip.

Here is a vivid way to see it. Carry a normal vector — an arrow pointing "outward" — along a closed path on each surface. On the cylinder, the arrow returns unchanged. On the Möbius strip, it comes back flipped.

§ 6 — The Klein Bottle & the Möbius Band

A surface with no inside

The Klein bottle, discovered by Felix Klein in 1882, is the simplest closed non-orientable surface. It can be built from a square by gluing: one pair of edges in the same direction (like a torus), and the other pair reversed. That single reversal changes everything.

Because it is non-orientable, the Klein bottle has no consistent "inside" and "outside." It cannot sit in three-dimensional space without crossing through itself — but in four dimensions, it embeds cleanly, with no self-intersection at all.

A twisted strip inside a doughnut

Take a solid torus — a filled doughnut — and embed a strip of paper inside it, running once around the core. If the strip has zero half-twists, it is an ordinary two-sided band: you can spread cream cheese on one side without getting any on the other. But give it one half-twist, and the strip becomes a Möbius band — it has only one side. If you try to spread cream cheese starting from any point, you will cover the entire strip in one continuous stroke. There is no "other side" left clean.

Use the − and + buttons to change the number of half-twists. Watch the strip change inside the torus. When the number of half-twists is odd, the strip is one-sided (a Möbius band). When it is even, the strip is two-sided (an ordinary band). The cream cheese indicator tells you which.

We now have the language: manifolds, genus, orientability, flat tori, Klein bottles. The two-dimensional case is fully classified. But the universe is three-dimensional — and that is where the real mystery begins.

§ 7 — Three-Manifolds

The shape of everything

The universe we inhabit is three-dimensional. At every point, you can move forward-back, left-right, up-down — three independent directions, spanning a local copy of \(\mathbb{R}^3\). But just as a two-dimensional being on a sphere cannot detect the curvature from inside a small patch, the global topology of our universe might be something far stranger than infinite Euclidean space.

A closed 3-manifold is a three-dimensional space that is finite in volume, has no boundary, and is locally indistinguishable from \(\mathbb{R}^3\). The question — which closed 3-manifold is our universe? — has haunted mathematics and cosmology for over a century.

In two dimensions, the classification was clean: genus and orientability. In three dimensions, the situation is incomparably richer. There is no single integer that classifies 3-manifolds. Instead, there is an enormous landscape of possibilities — organized by one of the deepest theorems in all of mathematics.

Thurston's Geometrization

In the 1970s, William Thurston proposed a breathtaking conjecture: every closed 3-manifold can be cut along certain spheres and tori into pieces, each of which admits one of exactly eight model geometries. Just as every surface is spherical, flat, or hyperbolic, every 3-manifold is built from eight types of geometry — no more, no fewer.

Thurston's Eight Geometries

Every closed 3-manifold decomposes into pieces, each carrying one of:

\(S^3\) · \(\mathbb{E}^3\) · \(\mathbb{H}^3\) · \(S^2 \!\times\! \mathbb{R}\) · \(\mathbb{H}^2 \!\times\! \mathbb{R}\) · \(\widetilde{\mathrm{SL}_2\mathbb{R}}\) · \(\mathrm{Nil}\) · \(\mathrm{Sol}\)

The first three are the constant-curvature geometries: positive (\(S^3\)), zero (\(\mathbb{E}^3\)), negative (\(\mathbb{H}^3\)). The remaining five are anisotropic — their geometry depends on direction.

\(S^3\) — Spherical geometry

Constant positive curvature. The geometry of the 3-sphere. Geodesics are great circles that always reconverge — walk in any direction and you return home. The simplest closed 3-manifold; Einstein's first model of the cosmos.

Three of these give the simplest candidates for the shape of the universe — the same three we met in § 2:

The three constant-curvature cosmologies

\(S^3\) — the 3-sphere  (positive curvature). The simplest closed 3-manifold. Walk far enough in any direction and you return home. Einstein's first cosmological model (1917).

\(T^3\) — the 3-torus  (zero curvature). A cube with opposite faces identified. Flat everywhere, finite volume, triply periodic. Look ahead and you see copies of yourself receding into the distance.

\(\mathcal{SW}\) — the Seifert–Weber space  (negative curvature). A regular dodecahedron with opposite faces identified by a \(3\pi/5\) twist. Parallel lines slowly diverge.

Perelman's proof

Thurston's conjecture remained unproved for nearly thirty years. Then, in 2002–2003, Grigori Perelman posted three papers to the arXiv that proved it in full — by completing Richard Hamilton's program of Ricci flow.

The idea: take a 3-manifold with an arbitrary metric and let it evolve under

$$\frac{\partial g}{\partial t} = -2\,\mathrm{Ric}(g)$$

This is a heat equation for geometry itself — bumps smooth out, curvature equilibrates. In two dimensions, Hamilton (1982) showed that any metric on \(S^2\) flows to a round sphere. In three dimensions, singularities form: necks pinch, topologies tear. Perelman showed how to perform surgery at each singularity — cut, cap, continue — and after finitely many surgeries, the manifold decomposes into exactly the geometric pieces Thurston predicted.

The proof also settled the Poincaré conjecture, the most famous open problem in topology: every simply connected closed 3-manifold is homeomorphic to \(S^3\). Perelman was offered the Fields Medal in 2006 and the Clay Millennium Prize in 2010. He declined both.

The cosmic question

Mathematics has given us the full classification. But which geometry is the universe?

The answer depends on measurement. The cosmic microwave background (CMB), the afterglow of the Big Bang, carries an imprint of the universe's global geometry. If space is positively curved, we should see certain patterns at large angular scales; if flat, different patterns; if negatively curved, yet others.

Current observations (WMAP, Planck) indicate that the universe is very close to flat — consistent with \(\mathbb{E}^3\), but also with a very large \(S^3\) or \(\mathbb{H}^3\). The error bars are too wide to distinguish. And even if space is exactly flat, we cannot tell whether it is infinite \(\mathbb{R}^3\) or a finite flat manifold like \(T^3\).

The question — is the universe round? — remains open.

Two sticks and a shadow were enough to measure the Earth.

The universe is still waiting for its Eratosthenes.
