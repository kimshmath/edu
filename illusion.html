§ 01 — The Depth That Disappears edu.kimsh.kr

Close One Eye

Two Universes, One Image

configuration 1 / 4 left: drag the observer 👁 around the solar system  |  right: what the observer sees

Close one eye. Hold your finger at arm's length. Now — without moving your head — can you tell exactly how far away it is?

You can't. And this is not a limitation of your eye. It is a fundamental fact about seeing itself. When the three-dimensional world lands on your retina, one entire dimension — depth — is silently destroyed. What remains is a flat painting. Two eyes can triangulate and recover some of what was lost. One eye receives only the painting.

Above is a tiny solar system — a sun and its planets, frozen in mid-orbit like a photograph. On the left, you see the true 3D arrangement from above. The golden eye is your observer — drag it around the solar system and watch how the view on the right changes. The right half shows what this observer actually sees: a flat image, all depth stripped away.

Now press Same Image.

Did you see that? The entire system just reshuffled. Some planets lunged closer and shrank. Others retreated and grew. The left panel is now completely different. But the right panel — the observer's view — did not change. Not a single line moved.

Try dragging the observer to a new position. The right panel updates: you are looking from a different place. But press Same Image again and — even from this new vantage point — the cosmos rearranges itself while the observer's picture stays perfectly still.

There are infinitely many different universes that produce the same image from any given viewpoint. This is why illusions are possible at all. Not because the eye is flawed, but because seeing necessarily destroys information.

But wait — go back to the observer and drag it slowly. Watch the right panel as you move. When the observer shifts position, nearby planets slide across the image much faster than distant ones. The sun barely moves; Mercury races. Even with one eye, a moving observer can sense depth — not from the image itself, but from how the image changes. This is called motion parallax, and it is one of the brain's most powerful tricks for recovering the dimension that projection destroys. We will return to it.

"The picture of everything that is illuminated and exists in front of a small hole in the wall of a darkened room will be found projected on the wall opposite the hole — inverted." — Ibn al-Haytham, Kitāb al-Manāẓir (c. 1011–1021 CE)
§ 02 — Where Parallel Lines Meet edu.kimsh.kr

The Vanishing Point

Drag the gold point — reshape the world

drag the vanishing point · watch every line obey

Stand on a long, straight road. Look into the distance. The two edges of the road — which you know are parallel — appear to converge. They seem to meet at a point on the horizon.

For two thousand years, painters struggled with this. They knew the lines were parallel. They could see them converge. How do you paint something that contradicts itself?

In 1425 Florence, an architect named Filippo Brunelleschi answered the question with an experiment. He stood at the door of the cathedral, painted the Baptistery on a small panel, drilled a hole through it, and held up a mirror. Looking through the hole from behind, with the mirror reflecting the painting — when painting and reality aligned, the viewer could not tell one from the other.

This was the first proof that a flat surface could perfectly simulate a three-dimensional scene. It worked because the painting implemented the same projection as the eye.

Ten years later, Leon Battista Alberti wrote down the rules. In De Pictura (1435), he described the picture plane as a transparent window between eye and scene — and showed that all parallel lines receding into depth must converge to a single vanishing point on the horizon.

Drag the golden point in the canvas above. Every line in the scene obeys it — the floor tiles, the columns, the ceiling beams. Move the point and the entire world reshapes itself, yet remains perfectly consistent. This is the power of the vanishing point: a single dot that dictates the geometry of an entire world.

Parallel lines do not meet in ordinary space. But in the image — in the projection — they do. The vanishing point is the image of a point at infinity: the place where the parallel lines would meet if you could walk forever. Projection makes the infinite visible.

"First of all, on the surface on which I am going to paint, I draw a rectangle of whatever size I want, which I regard as an open window through which the subject to be painted is seen." — Leon Battista Alberti, De Pictura (1435)
§ 03 — Freezing Light edu.kimsh.kr

Six Ways to
Remember a Moment

The camera obscura projected an image but could not hold it. For centuries, the most beautiful projections in the world appeared on darkened walls and vanished with the sun.

Then, one by one, we learned to freeze what projection creates — and each invention recovered something that the act of seeing had destroyed.

A photograph captures intensity. A stereo pair recovers depth. A film strip recovers time. And a hologram — alone among these — captures the complete wavefront: the amplitude and the phase of light at every point. Only a hologram can fool two eyes, a moving head, and a refocusing lens, all at once.

Each step in this history recovered one more dimension of reality that projection had thrown away. The photograph lives in two dimensions. Stereo adds a third. Cinema adds the fourth. Holography captures five of the seven dimensions of the plenoptic function — the complete description of every ray of light, everywhere, at every moment. Only reality itself fills all seven. — cf. Adelson & Bergen, "The Plenoptic Function and the Elements of Early Vision," 1991
§ 04 — Walking Inside a Painting edu.kimsh.kr

Walk Through
the Projection

Everything you have seen so far has been a picture about projection. Now step inside one. Below is a small world — a ground grid, cubes, pyramids, columns — all drawn by one single rule: every point in the scene is divided by how far away it is, and the result is a flat image. That is the entire trick.

Walk through it. Use the arrow keys or WASD to move, drag to look around, scroll to zoom. Watch how nearby objects rush past while distant ones barely move — this is the motion parallax we met in §01. Notice how the parallel lines of the grid converge toward a point on the horizon — the vanishing point from §02, now alive under your feet.

One rule: farther things look smaller

WASD / arrows: move · drag: look · scroll: zoom · Q/E: rise/descend

Try switching to Orthographic. The parallel lines suddenly stop converging. Distant objects are the same size as near ones. The grid becomes a rigid lattice with no depth cues at all. The only difference between these two modes is a single operation: dividing by depth. That one division is the entire difference between a flat blueprint and a world you can feel yourself standing inside.

Turn on Distance Fog. Objects fade as they recede — this is Leonardo da Vinci's sfumato, or what vision scientists call aerial perspective. Even on a flat screen, it adds an almost physical sense of space. Your brain accepts it as depth because in the real world, atmosphere scatters light, and distant things truly do fade. The fog is a lie — but a lie your visual system wants to believe.

§ 05 — Colors That Don't Exist edu.kimsh.kr

Metamers

The gold you see on this page does not exist. No photon of wavelength 580 nanometers left your screen. Your display emits only three wavelengths — red, green, blue — and your brain invented the gold by blending the responses of three types of cone cells.

Two physically different lights that produce the same cone response are called metamers. They look identical. They are not. You have never, in your entire life, seen a color. You have only ever seen equivalence classes of spectra — bundles of different lights that your three cone types cannot tell apart.

The canvas below shows this. On the left, three cone response curves sweep across the visible spectrum. On the right, for a selected color, two completely different spectral distributions are shown side by side — and they produce the exact same color swatch. Click anywhere on the spectrum bar to pick a new color.

If this reminds you of §01 — it should. There, infinitely many 3D worlds produced the same flat image. Here, infinitely many spectra produce the same color. Both are projections with vast kernels. Both destroy information that can never be recovered.

Same Color, Different Light

Your three cone types collapse the entire rainbow — an infinity of possible light mixtures — down to just three numbers. Countless different lights get folded into the same three-number reading. Every color you see is not a single light, but a whole family of lights that happen to feel the same to your eyes. If you read the drum chapter: this is the same story. Different realities, same observation.
§ 06 — Helmholtz's Unconscious Inference edu.kimsh.kr

The Guessing Machine

You are hallucinating right now. Constructively, coherently, usefully — but hallucinating. The image on your retina is flat. The world you experience is three-dimensional. That third dimension was constructed, in real time, by your visual cortex — a guess based on incomplete evidence.

Hermann von Helmholtz called this process unconscious inference in 1867: the brain draws conclusions about the world from ambiguous sensory data, using prior experience as a guide. Modern neuroscience restates this as Bayesian inference — the brain computes the most probable world given the image, weighted by what it already believes about how worlds tend to look.

An optical illusion occurs when this inference goes wrong — when the brain's expectations are so strong that they override what the image actually contains. Each illusion below exploits a specific assumption. Click any card to see the trick revealed.

The checker shadow illusion is perhaps the most devastating: two squares on a checkerboard, one in shadow, one in light, are physically identical in luminance. Your brain refuses to see them as the same — because in its model of the world, a white square in shadow should look darker than a dark square in light, and it adjusts your perception accordingly. The inference is correct in real life. Here, it produces an illusion.

§ 07 — The Room Where Giants Shrink edu.kimsh.kr

Projective Equivalence

In 1946, Adelbert Ames Jr. built a room. Through a peephole, it looks perfectly rectangular — four walls, a flat floor, right angles everywhere. A person stands in one corner. Another person stands in the opposite corner. One appears to be a giant. The other appears to be a dwarf. They are the same height.

The room is not rectangular. The floor slopes. The walls are trapezoidal. The ceiling tilts. But from that one viewpoint, through that one peephole, the distorted room projects to the exact same image as a normal room. The brain, trusting its prior that rooms are rectangular, sees a normal room with abnormally sized people.

Below, drag the slider to morph the room from rectangular to its true Ames shape — and watch the left panel (the peephole view) stay perfectly frozen while the right panel reveals the distortion. This is the final answer to the question in our title.

The Ames room is a constructive proof that the projection from three dimensions to two is not injective. Two different rooms — one normal, one warped — produce the same flat image. Close one eye, hold still, peer through a peephole, and the illusion becomes perfect.

So: are there perfect illusions?

For every flat image, infinitely many three-dimensional worlds could have produced it. Every photograph, every painting, every frame on your screen is a flat pattern that your brain, irresistibly, reads as depth. The illusion is not the exception. It is the universal condition of sight.

What saves us is redundancy: two eyes, a moving body, and an inference engine refined by five hundred million years of evolution. Close one eye, hold still, peer through a peephole — strip away the redundancy — and the illusion becomes perfect.

You have never seen the world. Only your brain's best guess. And that guess, miraculously, is almost always right.
