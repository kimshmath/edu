§ 1 — The Symplectic World

Area, not distance

In 1808, Joseph-Louis Lagrange discovered a geometric structure preserved by the equations of motion of classical mechanics. Two centuries later, this structure — the symplectic form — stands at the foundation of modern geometry and physics.

A symplectic manifold (M, ω) carries a closed, non-degenerate 2-form ω. Unlike a Riemannian metric, which measures lengths, the symplectic form measures oriented areas — the area swept out by the flow of a physical system through phase space.

ω = Σᵢ dpᵢ ∧ dqᵢ

Darboux's theorem says that every symplectic manifold looks locally like ℝ²ⁿ with this standard form. There are no local invariants: no curvature, no torsion. All the richness of symplectic geometry is global.

Hamiltonian flows preserve the symplectic form — this is Liouville's theorem. In the interactive, watch how shapes deform but their symplectic area ∫ω remains constant. This conservation is the geometric heart of classical mechanics.

§ 2 — Lagrangian Submanifolds

Where the symplectic form vanishes

An n-dimensional submanifold L inside a 2n-dimensional symplectic manifold (M, ω) is called Lagrangian if ω vanishes identically when restricted to L. These are the largest possible submanifolds on which the symplectic area form is trivial.

ω|L = 0,   dim L = ½ dim M

On the flat torus T² = ℝ²/ℤ² with ω = dp ∧ dq, every straight line of rational slope gives a closed Lagrangian circle. A line of slope a/b wraps a times around the p-direction and b times around the q-direction before closing up.

Two Lagrangian lines on T² with slopes a₁/b₁ and a₂/b₂ intersect in exactly |a₁b₂ − a₂b₁| points — the absolute value of the algebraic intersection number. This is a topological invariant, unchanged by Hamiltonian perturbation.

Arnold's conjecture (proved by Floer): the number of intersection points of L with its Hamiltonian translate is bounded below by the sum of Betti numbers of L. This is the foundational motivation for Floer homology.

§ 3 — Floer Homology

Counting holomorphic strips

Given two Lagrangians L₀, L₁ intersecting transversally, Andreas Floer (1988) constructed a chain complex whose generators are the intersection points and whose differential counts pseudo-holomorphic strips.

A pseudo-holomorphic strip is a map u : ℝ × [0,1] → M satisfying the Cauchy–Riemann equation ∂̄J u = 0 with boundary conditions: u(s,0) ∈ L₀ and u(s,1) ∈ L₁. As s → −∞, the strip converges to one intersection point p; as s → +∞, to another point q.

∂⟨p⟩ = Σq #𝓜(p,q) · ⟨q⟩

The miracle: ∂² = 0. Broken strips (two strips glued end-to-end) cancel in pairs, yielding a well-defined homology HF*(L₀, L₁) — the Floer homology.

The equation ∂̄J u = 0 is an elliptic PDE — a nonlinear generalization of the Cauchy–Riemann equations. Solutions are minimal-area surfaces: the "worldsheets" of open strings stretching between D-branes (= Lagrangians) in string theory.

§ 4 — The Fukaya Category

An algebra of Lagrangians

The Fukaya category Fuk(M) packages all Lagrangians and their Floer data into a single algebraic structure — an A∞-category — invented by Kenji Fukaya in 1993.

Objects: Lagrangian submanifolds (with extra data: grading, local system).

Morphisms: Hom(L₀, L₁) = CF*(L₀, L₁) — the Floer cochain complex.

Composition: the product μ² : CF*(L₀,L₁) ⊗ CF*(L₁,L₂) → CF*(L₀,L₂) counts pseudo-holomorphic triangles with boundary on L₀, L₁, L₂.

μk counts holomorphic (k+1)-gons
μ² = triangles,   μ³ = quadrilaterals,   …

The higher products μk satisfy A∞ relations — associativity holds up to coherent homotopy. The derived category DbFuk(M) is a triangulated category: the correct home for homological mirror symmetry.

For three Lagrangian lines on T² bounding a triangle of area A, the composition μ² picks up a factor of qA = e−A — an "instanton weight." These area-dependent corrections are the geometric origin of quantum effects in mirror symmetry.

§ 5 — The Mirror Conjecture

Two mountains, one valley

In his landmark address at the 1994 International Congress of Mathematicians in Zürich, Maxim Kontsevich proposed what may be the deepest structural duality in modern geometry: for a Calabi–Yau manifold X and its mirror partner Ŷ, the symplectic geometry of one is categorically equivalent to the algebraic geometry of the other.

DbFuk(X, ω)  ≅  DbCoh(Ŷ, J)
Homological Mirror Symmetry: the two sides of the mirror Lagrangians ↔ Sheaves · Floer homology ↔ Ext groups · J-holomorphic polygons ↔ Yoneda products

The left side — the Fukaya category DbFuk(X) — is built from symplectic geometry: Lagrangian submanifolds, Floer homology, pseudo-holomorphic curves. Its construction requires hard analysis: elliptic PDE theory, virtual fundamental cycles, Kuranishi structures.

The right side — the derived category DbCoh(Ŷ) — is built from algebraic geometry: vector bundles, coherent sheaves, Ext groups, derived functors. It is algebraic, combinatorial, and in many cases explicitly computable.

That two such utterly different mathematical traditions produce categorically equivalent outputs is the central mystery. For physicists, this is T-duality of Type IIA and Type IIB string theories compactified on mirror Calabi–Yau threefolds. Lagrangians are A-branes; coherent sheaves are B-branes.

A Calabi–Yau manifold is a Kähler manifold with vanishing first Chern class (equivalently, trivial canonical bundle). The mirror is another Calabi–Yau with the Hodge diamond "rotated": h1,1(X) = hn−1,1(Ŷ). The simplest examples are elliptic curves (tori) and K3 surfaces.

§ 6 — The Elliptic Curve

A complete example: self-mirror

The elliptic curve E ≅ T² is (essentially) its own mirror. This is the one case where HMS has been verified in complete detail, by Polishchuk–Zaslow (1998).

A Lagrangian line of slope a/b on T² (equipped with a flat U(1)-connection of holonomy ξ) corresponds under HMS to a stable vector bundle of rank b and degree a on the dual elliptic curve Ê — or, when b = 0, to a skyscraper sheaf supported at a point.

Line of slope a/b  ↔  stable bundle, rank b, degree a

The intersection number |a₁b₂ − a₂b₁| on the symplectic side becomes dim Ext⁰ — the dimension of the space of sheaf morphisms — on the algebraic side. Floer homology equals sheaf Ext groups.

This illustrates the SYZ philosophy: T² fibers over S¹ by projecting to one coordinate. Dualizing each S¹ fiber (replacing it with its Pontryagin dual) gives the mirror Ê. Lagrangian sections become line bundles on the dual — the Fourier–Mukai transform in action.

§ 7 — SYZ: The Geometric Origin of Mirrors

Dualizing the fibers

In 1996, Strominger, Yau, and Zaslow proposed a geometric explanation for why mirrors exist: a Calabi–Yau manifold X should admit a special Lagrangian torus fibration over a base B, and the mirror Ŷ is obtained by dualizing each torus fiber.

X → Tn B ← (Tn)∨ Ŷ

"Dualizing the fiber" means replacing each torus Tn = ℝn/ℤn by its Pontryagin dual (Tn)∨ = Hom(Tn, U(1)), which is again a torus of the same dimension. This interchanges complex and symplectic moduli.

At singular fibers — where the torus degenerates — the simple dualization breaks down and must be corrected by quantum corrections (disc instantons). Understanding these corrections is the central challenge of the Gross–Siebert program and tropical geometry.

For the quintic threefold in ℂP⁴, the SYZ fibration exists near the "large complex structure limit." The singular fibers form a trivalent graph on B ≅ S³ — the discriminant locus. The Gross–Siebert program reconstructs the mirror from tropical data on this graph.

§ 8 — Enumerative Miracles

2,875 lines on the quintic

Fields Medal · 1998
Maxim Kontsevich
1964 –  ·  Khimki → IHÉS

"There should exist some equivalence of triangulated categories…"
— Architect of homological mirror symmetry. His 1994 ICM address unified symplectic topology, algebraic geometry, and string theory into a single categorical framework, reshaping the landscape of modern mathematics.

The most stunning application of mirror symmetry: computing Gromov–Witten invariants — counts of pseudo-holomorphic curves — from the periods of the mirror.

The quintic threefold X ⊂ ℂP⁴ (defined by a homogeneous polynomial of degree 5) is a Calabi–Yau threefold. How many rational curves (holomorphic maps ℂP¹ → X) of degree d does it contain?

In 1991, Candelas, de la Ossa, Green, and Parkes used the mirror of the quintic to predict these counts — before mathematicians had any tools to verify them directly:

N₁ = 2,875
N₂ = 609,250
N₃ = 317,206,375
N₄ = 242,467,530,000
N₅ = 229,305,888,887,625

The degree-1 count (2,875 lines on a generic quintic) was known since the 19th century via Schubert calculus. The degree-2 count had been verified by Katz (1986). But degree 3 onward were new predictions — later confirmed by Givental and Lian–Liu–Yau.

The mirror computation reduces curve counting to a period integral: solve a Picard–Fuchs differential equation on the moduli space of the mirror family Ŷψ = {x₁⁵ + … + x₅⁵ − 5ψ x₁…x₅ = 0}/(ℤ/5ℤ)³, then read off the Gromov–Witten invariants from the Taylor expansion of the "mirror map."

§ 9 — A Living Field

Open frontiers

Beyond Calabi–Yau

Kontsevich's conjecture applies to Calabi–Yau manifolds, but extensions to Fano and general type varieties are now active research frontiers. For Fano manifolds, the mirror is a Landau–Ginzburg model — a non-compact manifold with a holomorphic function W (the "superpotential"). The Fukaya category is replaced by the Fukaya–Seidel category of Lefschetz thimbles.

Wrapped Fukaya categories

For non-compact symplectic manifolds, the wrapped Fukaya category allows Lagrangians to "wrap" infinitely near the boundary. Its endomorphism algebra recovers symplectic cohomology — an invariant with deep connections to loop spaces and string topology.

Connections across mathematics

Mirror symmetry now reaches into seemingly distant fields: geometric Langlands (where homological mirror symmetry for the Hitchin fibration gives an approach to Langlands duality), knot theory (where Lagrangian Floer theory in symmetric products recovers Khovanov homology), and cluster algebras (where mutations correspond to Lagrangian surgery).
