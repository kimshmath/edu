§ 2 — Enter the Third

The clock shatters

In 1887, King Oscar II of Sweden offered a prize for proving the solar system is stable. Henri Poincaré submitted a brilliant 270-page memoir claiming to prove stability — and won. The paper was sent to the printers.

Then, while reviewing the proofs, his colleague Lars Phragmén found a mistake. Poincaré re-examined his own work and realized, to his horror, that the error was not a typo — it reversed his conclusion entirely. The trajectories he had dismissed as impossible were in fact everywhere. Tiny changes in starting position led to wildly different futures. He had discovered chaos.

Poincaré recalled every printed copy at his own expense — the cost exceeded the prize money — and published a corrected memoir proving the opposite of what he had originally claimed. It became one of the most important papers in the history of mathematics.

Why "chaos" is a precise word. Poincaré didn't mean randomness. He meant sensitive dependence on initial conditions: two systems starting a hair's breadth apart end up in completely different states. The laws are deterministic — the same inputs always produce the same outputs — but the slightest uncertainty in measurement gets amplified beyond all prediction.
§ 3 — Lagrange Points

Five islands of calm

Even in the chaos, there are sanctuaries. In 1772, Lagrange proved that a small object can balance in exactly five positions relative to two large orbiting bodies. Three lie along the line connecting them. Two form equilateral triangles — one leading, one trailing — and these are stable.

The James Webb Space Telescope sits at one of these points right now, 1.5 million kilometers behind Earth, balanced in the gravitational landscape like a marble at the bottom of a bowl. Click anywhere below to drop a test particle and feel the topology of gravity.

§ 4 — The Figure Eight

A dance hidden for three centuries

In 1993, Cristopher Moore found something astonishing on a computer: three equal masses chasing each other along a single figure-eight curve, each body tracing the exact same path, equally spaced like beads on a wire. Chenciner and Montgomery proved it rigorously in 2000.

§ 5 — Is the Solar System Stable?

Four billion years of luck

This is the oldest open question in mathematical physics. Laplace believed the solar system was eternal clockwork. Poincaré showed it might not be. In 2009, Jacques Laskar ran thousands of simulations over five billion years. In roughly 1% of them, Mercury's orbit stretched until it crossed Venus's path — collision, or ejection into deep space.

Our solar system is not a clock. It is a slow game of chance, and we are inside it. Below, the inner solar system fast-forwards through millions of years. Most runs are quiet. Some are not. Watch Mercury's eccentricity gauge on the right — and hope it stays below the red line.

1% in five billion years. Laskar's simulations showed that Mercury is the solar system's wild card. Its orbit is chaotic on timescales of millions of years — deterministic but unpredictable, just as Poincaré discovered. We have been lucky so far.

Two bodies dance in eternal ellipses. Add a third, and the future becomes a question that no amount of computation can fully answer.

And yet — inside the chaos, a figure-eight. Among the instabilities, five still points. Across four billion years of dice-rolls, a solar system that held. The three-body problem is not a failure of physics. It is where physics meets its own horizon.
