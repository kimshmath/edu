Chapter 0

현실을 산산조각 낸 실험

In 1801, Thomas Young passed light through two narrow slits and saw something impossible: an interference pattern — proof that light is a wave. A century later, physicists repeated the experiment firing one photon at a time. Each photon landed as a single dot. But as thousands accumulated, the interference pattern emerged again.

A single particle, somehow interfering with itself. Things got stranger: when they placed a detector at one slit to see which way the photon went, the pattern vanished. Observation itself changed the outcome.

광자: 0

"양자역학을 이해했다고 생각한다면, 양자역학을 이해하지 못한 것이다."

— 리처드 파인만

왜 이것이 중요한가

The double-slit experiment is not just a curious lab trick. It tells us that the very fabric of nature is probabilistic. Particles do not have definite trajectories between measurements. What we call "reality" only crystallizes in the act of observation. This is not philosophy — it is what the mathematics demands.

Chapter 1

우주는 완벽했다

For two hundred years, physics was a triumph of certainty. Newton's laws could predict the motion of every planet, every cannonball, every grain of sand — if you knew the starting conditions. The universe was a clockwork machine, and the laws of physics were its gears.

공을 드래그하고 놓아 발사하세요 — 완벽한 뉴턴 역학

Every collision obeys conservation of momentum and energy exactly. No surprises. No randomness. Given the positions and velocities of every particle in the universe, the entire future is already determined.

"An intellect which at a certain moment would know all forces… and all positions of all items of which nature is composed — nothing would be uncertain, and the future just like the past would be present before its eyes."

— 피에르-시몽 라플라스, 1814

This hypothetical being — Laplace's demon — could, in principle, compute the future of the entire cosmos from a single snapshot. Free will, surprise, novelty: all illusions in a deterministic universe.

But there is another demon in physics — one that attacks from the opposite direction. In 1867, James Clerk Maxwell imagined a tiny creature guarding a door between two chambers of gas. By selectively opening and closing the door, the demon could sort fast molecules to one side and slow ones to the other — creating a temperature difference from nothing, seemingly violating the second law of thermodynamics.

Left: --   Right: --

Gas molecules bounce between two chambers. Wake the demon — it opens the door only for fast molecules going right and slow molecules going left. Watch the right side heat up and the left side cool down — for free. Or so it seems.

두 악마, 두 세계관

Laplace's demon knows everything and computes the future. It embodies classical determinism: the universe is a clockwork, and prediction is merely a matter of computation. Maxwell's demon knows only one thing — which molecules are fast — and uses that knowledge to cheat thermodynamics. It was eventually defeated: Rolf Landauer showed in 1961 that erasing the demon's memory (which it must do eventually) generates entropy. Information is physical, and forgetting has a thermodynamic cost. The second law survives — but only because information and entropy are deeply connected. This insight would later become a cornerstone of quantum information theory.

This was the world that physicists believed in at the dawn of the twentieth century. Elegant. Complete. Beautiful.

And then, on December 14, 1900, a quiet German physicist named Max Planck stood before the Berlin Physical Society and, almost reluctantly, broke it.

무엇이 잘못되었는가?

Classical physics could not explain the glow of a hot oven. The equations predicted that a heated cavity should radiate infinite energy at high frequencies — the "ultraviolet catastrophe." Planck's radical fix: energy is not continuous. It comes in discrete packets — quanta. That single word would unravel two centuries of certainty.

Chapter 2

물리학을 깨뜨린 오븐

In 1859, Gustav Kirchhoff posed a challenge: determine the spectrum of light emitted by a heated cavity. By the 1890s, experimentalists had mapped the curve precisely. But no theory could explain it.

Classical physics made a terrifying prediction. The Rayleigh–Jeans law — derived honestly from Maxwell's electromagnetism and Boltzmann's thermodynamics — said that the energy radiated should grow without bound at high frequencies. A warm oven should emit infinite energy in the ultraviolet. This was absurd. The physicist Paul Ehrenfest gave it a name: the ultraviolet catastrophe.

A heated cavity. Photons bounce inside, reaching thermal equilibrium. As temperature rises, the color shifts from red to orange to white to blue — and higher-energy photons appear. Drag the slider to heat it up.

On the evening of October 19, 1900, Max Planck found a formula that fit the experimental data perfectly. But there was a catch: to derive it, he had to assume that energy is emitted not in a continuous stream, but in discrete lumps — quanta — each carrying energy proportional to its frequency.

E = hν

Energy equals Planck's constant times frequency.
The equation that started a revolution — December 14, 1900.

Planck himself did not believe nature was truly quantized. He called it "an act of desperation." He spent the next decade trying to undo his own discovery — to re-derive his formula without quanta. He failed.

The blue curve is Planck's quantum formula — it matches experiment perfectly. Toggle the classical prediction to see it diverge to infinity. That divergence is the catastrophe.

"Briefly summarized, what I did can be described as simply an act of desperation."

— 막스 플랑크, R.W. Wood에게 보낸 편지, 1931

Five years later, a young patent clerk in Bern took Planck's quanta seriously. Einstein proposed that light itself comes in particles — photons. His explanation of the photoelectric effect proved it: below a certain frequency, no amount of intense light can eject electrons from a metal. Only individual photons with enough energy can do the job.

Shine light on a metal plate. Below the threshold frequency, no electrons are ejected — no matter how bright the light. Above the threshold, even dim light ejects electrons. Frequency matters, not intensity. This is what won Einstein his Nobel Prize.

마지못한 혁명가와 대담한 혁명가

Planck introduced quanta as a mathematical trick and spent years trying to take it back. Einstein took the idea literally and changed physics forever. Their contrast reveals something about how science works: sometimes the right idea appears before anyone — even its discoverer — is ready to believe it. Einstein's Nobel Prize in 1921 was not for relativity. It was for the photoelectric effect — for showing that light is made of particles.

Chapter 3

더 많이 알수록, 더 적게 알게 된다

Imagine trying to locate an electron. You shine light on it. But light carries momentum — to see something small, you need short-wavelength (high-energy) light, which kicks the electron unpredictably. Use gentle long-wavelength light, and you cannot see where it is. This was Heisenberg's gamma-ray microscope thought experiment, published in 1927.

But the uncertainty principle goes deeper than clumsy measurements. It is not that we disturb the particle by looking. The particle genuinely does not have a definite position and momentum simultaneously. The wave function — the mathematical object that describes a quantum particle — simply cannot be both narrow in position and narrow in momentum at the same time.

Δx · Δp ≥ ℏ/2

Left: position space — where the particle might be.   Right: momentum space — how fast it might be going.
Squeeze one and watch the other spread. This is Fourier duality made visible.

The mathematical reason is beautiful: position and momentum are Fourier duals. A wave tightly peaked in position is built from many different frequencies — many different momenta. A wave with a single definite momentum is a perfect sine wave, spread across all of space. You cannot have both. This is the same mathematics behind MP3 compression, MRI imaging, and the bandwidth theorem in telecommunications.

"The more precisely the position is determined, the less precisely the momentum is known in this instant, and vice versa."

— 베르너 하이젠베르크, 1927

A quantum particle confined to a box. Make the box smaller — the particle's position is more certain, but it bounces faster and more wildly. Confine it tightly and its kinetic energy skyrockets. Toggle to see the standing wave that the particle actually is.

Einstein never accepted the uncertainty principle as fundamental. He believed it concealed a deeper, deterministic reality — that there were "hidden variables" we hadn't found yet. At the 1927 Solvay Conference, he challenged Bohr with ever-more-clever thought experiments. Bohr refuted each one — once, famously, using Einstein's own general relativity against him.

"신은 우주와 주사위 놀이를 하지 않는다."

— 알베르트 아인슈타인

"아인슈타인, 신에게 이래라저래라 하지 마시오."

— 닐스 보어의 유명한 대답

진공은 비어 있지 않다

The uncertainty principle applies to all conjugate pairs — including energy and time. A system can briefly violate conservation of energy by borrowing ΔE for a time Δt, as long as ΔE · Δt ≥ ℏ/2. This means the vacuum itself seethes with virtual particles — electron-positron pairs flickering in and out of existence for unimaginably brief instants. This is not speculation: the Casimir effect (1948) and the Lamb shift are direct, measured consequences of vacuum fluctuations. Empty space is the most violent place in the universe.

Chapter 4

동시에 앞면이자 뒷면인 동전

Imagine flipping a coin that is, genuinely, both heads and tails at once — not because you haven't looked, but because it has no single value until you do. A quantum particle in a superposition does not simply have an unknown state. It exists in all possible states simultaneously. When we measure, the superposition collapses to a single definite outcome.

In 1922, Otto Stern and Walther Gerlach sent silver atoms through an inhomogeneous magnetic field. Classically, the atoms should have spread into a continuous smear. Instead, they split into exactly two beams — spin up and spin down. Nature gives only discrete answers.

The Bloch sphere is the mathematician's way of visualizing a quantum bit — a qubit. The north pole is state |0⟩, the south pole is |1⟩, and every other point on the sphere is a superposition of both. The angle determines the probability of each outcome.

This is the measurement problem — arguably the deepest unsolved puzzle in all of physics. The Schrödinger equation evolves the wave function smoothly and deterministically. But when we "measure," the function suddenly jumps to a definite value. What triggers this jump? What counts as a measurement? Does the wave function really collapse, or does something else happen?

"양자역학을 이해하는 사람은 아무도 없다."

— 리처드 파인만, 물리 법칙의 특성, 1965

Erwin Schrödinger devised his famous thought experiment in 1935 — not to celebrate quantum weirdness, but to ridicule it. A cat in a sealed box is entangled with a radioactive atom. If the atom decays, poison is released. Quantum mechanics says the atom is in a superposition of decayed and not-decayed. Therefore the cat is simultaneously alive and dead. Schrödinger found this conclusion absurd — and that was exactly his point.

"One can even set up quite ridiculous cases. A cat is penned up in a steel chamber…"

— 에르빈 슈뢰딩거, 1935

Eugene Wigner pushed this further with Wigner's friend: if a physicist inside a lab measures the cat, they see a definite result. But from outside, Wigner describes the entire lab — physicist included — as being in superposition. Who is right? When does collapse happen? This paradox remains unanswered. A 2019 experiment by Proietti et al. demonstrated that two observers can indeed have contradictory yet internally consistent records of the same quantum event.

양자역학의 난제

The measurement problem is sometimes called the "hard problem" of physics, by analogy with the hard problem of consciousness. In both cases, we have successful mathematical descriptions (the Schrödinger equation; neuroscience) that seem to leave out something essential about subjective experience. Some physicists — including Penrose and Wigner himself — have speculated that consciousness and quantum mechanics are connected. Most physicists are skeptical, but the question refuses to go away. What is observation?

Chapter 5

으스스한 원격 작용

In May 1935, Einstein, Podolsky, and Rosen published a paper with an innocent title and an explosive argument. Two particles, once entangled, remain correlated no matter how far apart. Measure one — the other's state is instantly determined. Einstein saw only two options: either information travels faster than light (impossible), or the particles always "knew" their values in advance through hidden variables. He concluded quantum mechanics must be incomplete.

Bohr's response, published months later, was characteristically opaque. He argued that one cannot speak of the properties of a particle independently of the measurement context. Einstein was unconvinced. The debate seemed philosophical, untestable — until John Bell, an Irish physicist working at CERN, changed everything.

Create an entangled pair. The particles fly apart in superposition (shown as both colors). Alice 입자 측정 — and watch Bob's instantly resolve to the correlated state, no matter the distance.

In 1964, Bell proved a theorem of extraordinary elegance and power. He showed that if hidden variables exist, then the correlations between entangled particles must satisfy a mathematical inequality. Quantum mechanics predicts correlations that violate this inequality. The question was no longer philosophical — it was experimental.

"I cannot seriously believe in [quantum mechanics] because the theory cannot be reconciled with the idea that physics should represent a reality in time and space, free from spooky actions at a distance."

— 알베르트 아인슈타인, 막스 보른에게 보낸 편지, 1947

The Bell test. Alice and Bob each choose a measurement angle for their entangled photon. Quantum mechanics predicts correlation = cos²(θA − θB). Hidden variable theories predict less. Try 0° vs 22.5°, then 0° vs 67.5° — watch the Bell inequality break.

Alain Aspect performed the first convincing Bell test in Paris in 1982, switching detector settings while the photons were already in flight — closing the "locality loophole." In 2015, Hensen et al. at Delft achieved the first fully loophole-free Bell test, confirming the violation with no possible classical escape. Nature is not locally real.

"The universe is not only queerer than we suppose, but queerer than we can suppose."

— J.B.S. 홀데인 (양자 맥락에서 자주 인용)

There is one remaining loophole: superdeterminism. If the entire universe — including Alice and Bob's "free" choices of measurement angles — was determined from the Big Bang onward, then Bell's theorem does not apply. This is logically possible but philosophically devastating: it would mean no experiment can ever teach us anything, because every experimental choice was predetermined. Most physicists reject it — but it cannot be disproven.

2022년 노벨상

Alain Aspect, John Clauser, and Anton Zeilinger were awarded the 2022 Nobel Prize in Physics "for experiments with entangled photons, establishing the violation of Bell inequalities and pioneering quantum information science." Their work confirmed that entanglement is not just a theoretical curiosity — it is a resource. Quantum teleportation, quantum cryptography, and quantum computing all depend on this "spooky action" being real.

Chapter 6

이 모든 것은 무엇을 의미하는가?

Quantum mechanics is, by any measure, the most successful theory in the history of science. It predicts the behavior of atoms, molecules, semiconductors, lasers, and nuclear reactions with staggering accuracy. But after a century, physicists still cannot agree on what the theory says about reality.

The fight began at the fifth Solvay Conference in October 1927 — perhaps the most extraordinary gathering of minds in history. Twenty-nine physicists, seventeen of whom were or would become Nobel laureates, met in Brussels to discuss the newly born quantum theory. Einstein and Bohr squared off. The debate would define physics for the next century.

사고 실험: 양자 텔레포테이션

Can you transmit a quantum state from one place to another, without physically moving the particle? In 1993, Charles Bennett and colleagues proved it is possible — using entanglement as a resource and classical communication as a channel. The original particle's state is destroyed at the source and recreated at the destination. Nothing travels faster than light — yet the quantum information has been teleported.

Step 1: Alice and Bob share an entangled pair. Step 2: Alice measures her particle together with the state she wants to send — this destroys her copy. Step 3: Alice sends Bob 2 classical bits. Bob applies a correction — and now holds the exact original state. No cloning. No faster-than-light. Just entanglement doing its magic.

Why it matters

Quantum teleportation does not move matter or energy — it moves information. The no-cloning theorem guarantees that the original is always destroyed: you cannot copy a quantum state. This is not science fiction teleportation, but something subtler and arguably more profound — a proof that entanglement is a genuine physical resource, as real as energy or momentum. Teleportation was first demonstrated experimentally by Zeilinger's group in 1997 and is now a routine operation in quantum computing labs. It is the backbone of quantum networks and the future quantum internet.

사고 실험: 양자 지우개

John Wheeler's delayed-choice experiment asks: can a choice made after a photon has already been detected change whether it behaved as a wave or a particle? Astonishingly, yes. In the quantum eraser variant (Scully & Drühl, 1982), which-way information is recorded — destroying interference. But if you later erase that information, the interference pattern reappears in the data. The future seems to reach back and reshape the past.

광자: 0

No info: interference pattern forms. 경로 기록: pattern vanishes — just two blobs. 기록 삭제: the interference pattern comes back — as if the photons "knew" you would erase. The choice can be made after the photons have already landed.

"No elementary quantum phenomenon is a phenomenon until it is a registered phenomenon — that is to say, brought to a close by an irreversible act of amplification."

— 존 아치볼드 휠러

In a 2013 poll of 33 physicists at a quantum foundations conference (Schlosshauer, Kofler & Zeilinger), the results were striking: Copenhagen 42%, Information-based 24%, Many Worlds 18%, other 16%. There was no majority. The measurement problem remains, in the words of physicist Steven Weinberg, "the most important unsolved problem in the foundations of physics."

논쟁의 미래

The debate is not merely philosophical — it shapes research programs. Decoherence theory explains why superpositions vanish at macroscopic scales, but does not solve the measurement problem. Quantum Darwinism (Zurek) asks how objective classical reality emerges from a quantum universe. Objective collapse theories (Penrose, Ghirardi–Rimini–Weber) modify the Schrödinger equation to make collapse physical and testable. And the quest for quantum gravity — unifying quantum mechanics with general relativity — may ultimately demand we revise our understanding of space, time, and the nature of observation itself. The story of quantum mechanics is far from over.
