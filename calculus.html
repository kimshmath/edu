§ 1 — Zeno's Arrow

The arrow that cannot move

"At every instant, the arrow occupies a fixed position. A thing in a fixed position is at rest. So when does it move?" — Zeno of Elea, c. 450 BC

The paradox haunted Western thought for over two thousand years. Aristotle tried to refute it. He failed. So did everyone else — until Newton and Leibniz, in the late 1600s, discovered the answer.

Don't ask what the arrow does at a single instant. Instead, look at two nearby moments, measure how far the arrow moves between them, and divide by the time elapsed. Then shrink that gap. The ratio settles to a definite number — the instantaneous velocity. The arrow was never frozen. We just needed the right way to see it.

Calculus refuted a metaphysics. Zeno's paradoxes weren't puzzles — they were Parmenides' argument that all change is illusion. The limit dissolved the paradox and, with it, a philosophy.

§ 3 — The Brachistochrone Race

Can you beat a 330-year-old curve?

In June 1696, Johann Bernoulli posed a challenge to Europe's mathematicians: what curve carries a bead from A to B in the least time under gravity? Newton received the problem at 4 pm, solved it by 4 am, and sent the solution anonymously. Bernoulli recognized him anyway: "I know the lion by its claw."

The answer is a cycloid — the curve traced by a point on a rolling wheel. It dips steeply, gaining speed before straightening out. It looks reckless. It always wins. Build your own ramp and race it.

§ 4 — Filling the Gap

How to measure the unmeasurable

Archimedes could find the area under a parabola — but it took him a lifetime of ingenuity. Riemann's idea, twenty-one centuries later, was almost rude in its simplicity: chop the region into thin rectangles and add up their areas. The thinner the slices, the better the answer.

§ 5 — The Infinite Staircase

Some infinities fit

Add 1 + ½ + ⅓ + ¼ + … forever. The sum grows without bound — slowly, but inexorably. The tower has no ceiling. This is the harmonic series, and its divergence stunned 14th-century mathematicians.

Now try 1 + ¼ + ⅑ + ¹⁄₁₆ + … — the reciprocals of perfect squares. The tower stops. It converges. And it converges to exactly π²/6. Why should π — the ratio of a circle's circumference to its diameter — appear in a sum of fractions? Euler solved this in 1735 and it made him famous across Europe. Everything moves. The question was never whether, but how — and the answer turned out to be a limit, a slope, a curve you sculpt with your own hand.

A ball on a hill. A bead on a wire that always loses to a 330-year-old cycloid. Rectangles that dissolve into area. Blocks that somehow stack to π. This is calculus — the mathematics of change, of motion, of everything that refuses to stand still.
