05 — LEBESGUE'S RULES

Back to First Principles

Around 1902, Henri Lebesgue asked: what should volume mean? He wrote down four simple rules — axioms — that any reasonable notion of volume must satisfy.

AXIOM 1

Empty → Zero

The empty set has volume 0. Nothing weighs nothing.

AXIOM 2

The Unit Cube

A 1×1×1 cube has volume exactly 1. This is our yardstick.

AXIOM 3

Finite Additivity

Split a shape into two non-overlapping pieces: the total volume is the sum of the parts.

AXIOM 4

Countable Additivity

Even for countably infinitely many pieces (1st, 2nd, 3rd, …), the total is the sum. This is the key axiom — inherited from the tradition of Riemann sums.

Lebesgue's theory assigns volume to almost every set you can think of. Points, lines, surfaces — even exotic fractal sets. Mathematicians celebrated: the problem of volume was solved.

Was it, though?

06 — VITALI'S BOMBSHELL

The Set with No Volume

In 1905, Giuseppe Vitali asked a devastating question: can we assign a volume to every subset of [0, 1] while obeying Lebesgue's four rules? His answer was no — and the proof is shockingly simple.

Building the Vitali Set

1

Group by rational distance. Say two numbers in [0,1] are "equivalent" if their difference is rational. This splits [0,1] into uncountably many classes — clouds of points that are rational distances apart.

2

Pick one from each class. Using the Axiom of Choice, select exactly one representative from each equivalence class. This collection V is the Vitali set.

3

Shift and cover. Translate V by every rational number in [−1, 1]. These countably many copies of V cover [0,1] without overlapping.

💥

The contradiction. If V has volume 0, then countably many copies add to 0 — but they cover [0,1], which has volume 1. If V has volume ε > 0, then countably many copies add to ∞ — but they fit inside [−1, 2], which has volume 3.

Either way: contradiction. V cannot have any volume at all.

"Not every set can be measured. Lebesgue's dream is shattered." — The lesson of the Vitali set, 1905

The culprit? The Axiom of Choice — the same axiom that powers the Banach–Tarski paradox. Without it, Vitali's argument cannot even begin: you need it to pick one representative from each of uncountably many classes.

After Vitali, mathematicians accepted a sobering truth: not every subset of space has a volume. Only the "nice" sets — the measurable ones — can be measured. The rest are mathematical ghosts: they exist (if you accept AC), but they have no size.

CODA — WHAT COMES NEXT

From No Volume to Paradox

Vitali proved that countably infinite pieces can break volume. But what if you only use finitely many pieces? Surely that's safe?

In 1924, Stefan Banach and Alfred Tarski showed that the answer is no — spectacularly, terrifyingly no. With just five pieces, you can take a ball the size of a bean and reassemble it into a ball the size of the Sun.
