Section 01

A Coin That Refuses to Choose

선택을 거부하는 동전

In 1927, physicists at Bell Labs fired electrons — one at a time — through a barrier with two thin slits. Each electron landed as a single dot on a detector screen behind the barrier. Nothing strange about that. But after thousands of electrons had arrived, the dots formed a pattern of bright and dark bands — the signature of wave interference. Each electron had somehow passed through both slits simultaneously and interfered with itself.

This is not a metaphor. It is not an approximation. The mathematics of quantum mechanics says, with absolute precision, that before measurement a particle exists in a superposition of states — it genuinely does not have a definite position. The act of measuring forces a choice. Physicists call this collapse.

"If you think you understand quantum mechanics, you don't understand quantum mechanics."

— Richard Feynman

The double-slit experiment is the heart of quantum strangeness. Feynman called it the experiment that contains "the only mystery" of quantum mechanics. Every other quantum phenomenon — entanglement, tunnelling, teleportation — is a variation on this theme: nature keeps its options open until forced to decide.

The equation that governs this pattern is elegant. For two narrow slits separated by distance d, illuminated by a wave of wavelength λ, the probability of landing at angle θ from the centre is:

P(θ) ∝ cos²(π d sin θ / λ) × sinc²(π a sin θ / λ)

The first factor creates the fringes — the interference between two slits. The second factor is the envelope — the diffraction from each slit's finite width a. Together, they produce the beautiful striped pattern you see above. Every bright band is a place where the waves from both slits arrive in phase. Every dark band is where they cancel.

But here is what makes this truly strange: the pattern appears even when electrons are fired one at a time. No electron "knows" about any other electron. Each one interferes with itself. The interference is not between particles — it is between possibilities.

When we place a detector at the slits — asking which slit did you go through? — the interference pattern vanishes. The fringes dissolve into two bland humps, one behind each slit, exactly as classical particles would produce. Knowing the path destroys the quantum behaviour. Information and interference are locked in a fundamental trade-off that sits at the heart of quantum mechanics — and, as we will see, at the heart of quantum computing.

Section 02

Spooky Dice

으스스한 주사위

In 1935, Albert Einstein published a paper with Boris Podolsky and Nathan Rosen arguing that quantum mechanics must be incomplete. Their reasoning was simple: if you prepare two particles together and then separate them by any distance — across a room or across a galaxy — quantum mechanics predicts that measuring one particle instantly determines the state of the other. Einstein called this "spooky action at a distance" and refused to believe it.

His intuition was natural. Surely, he argued, the particles must carry hidden instructions from the moment they separate — like two sealed envelopes, each containing the same letter. Opening one envelope in Tokyo tells you what the other says in Paris, with no magic required. This idea is called a local hidden variable theory.

In 1964, the Irish physicist John Bell proved Einstein wrong — not with an experiment, but with mathematics. He showed that any local hidden variable theory must obey a certain inequality. Quantum mechanics violates that inequality. Nature really is spooky.

"The EPR paradox is resolved in the way which Einstein would have liked least."

— John Stewart Bell, 1964

The mathematics behind this is Bell's inequality, specifically the CHSH inequality formulated by Clauser, Horne, Shimony, and Holt in 1969. For any classical (local hidden variable) strategy, a certain combination of correlations S satisfies:

|S| ≤ 2 (classical limit) |S| ≤ 2√2 ≈ 2.83 (quantum maximum)

The number 2√2 is called Tsirelson's bound. It is the absolute maximum that quantum mechanics allows — and experiments consistently reach it. This is not a loophole or an artifact. Dozens of experiments since Alain Aspect's landmark 1982 test have confirmed the violation with increasing rigour, culminating in three loophole-free Bell tests in 2015 that closed every known escape route for classical explanations.

What entanglement means, precisely, is this: two particles can share correlations stronger than any classical mechanism can produce. Not because they communicate faster than light — quantum mechanics does not allow sending information this way. But because their joint state is a single, indivisible quantum object, even when the particles are light-years apart. Measuring one does not "send a signal" to the other; instead, they were never truly separate to begin with.

This is the resource that powers quantum computing. Entanglement allows qubits to share information in ways that have no classical analogue — and it is the reason a quantum computer with n entangled qubits can, in principle, explore 2n possibilities simultaneously.

Section 03

The Qubit Globe

큐비트 지구본

A classical bit is the simplest object in computing: it is either 0 or 1. A light switch, a coin lying flat. A qubit — the quantum bit — is something far richer. Its state is not a single value but a point on the surface of a sphere. Every direction on that sphere is a valid quantum state. This sphere is called the Bloch sphere, and it is the fundamental geometry of quantum information.

The north pole of the Bloch sphere represents the state |0⟩. The south pole represents |1⟩. Everything in between is a superposition — the qubit is partly 0 and partly 1, with a specific phase relationship between the two. The equator holds states that are equal parts 0 and 1 but differ in phase: |+⟩, |−⟩, |i⟩, |−i⟩.

|ψ⟩ = cos(θ/2) |0⟩ + e^(iφ) sin(θ/2) |1⟩

This is the most important equation in quantum computing. The angles θ and φ are the latitude and longitude on the Bloch sphere. Every single-qubit quantum gate — every operation you can perform on a qubit — is a rotation of this sphere.

The Hadamard gate (H) is perhaps the most important single gate: it rotates |0⟩ to the equator, creating an equal superposition of 0 and 1. This is how quantum computers begin most computations — by putting qubits into superposition, opening up the space of possibilities.

The X gate flips |0⟩ to |1⟩ and vice versa — it is the quantum analogue of a NOT gate. The Z gate leaves |0⟩ alone but flips the phase of |1⟩, rotating around the vertical axis. The T gate is a subtle rotation by π/8 around that same axis. These are the building blocks: any single-qubit operation can be composed from H and T gates alone.

"A classical bit is a light switch. A qubit is a globe — every point on its surface is a valid state."

— common analogy in quantum information

With just 4 qubits, a quantum computer tracks 16 complex amplitudes — easy for a laptop. At 20 qubits: over a million. At 40 qubits: over a trillion, requiring terabytes of memory. At 50 qubits, no classical computer on Earth can fully simulate the system. At 300 qubits, the number of amplitudes exceeds the number of atoms in the observable universe. This is the source of quantum computing's power — and its mystery.

Section 04

The Geometry of Maybe

가능성의 기하학

Superposition gives a quantum computer access to an exponentially large space. Entanglement lets qubits share information without classical analogues. But neither, on its own, explains why quantum computers are faster. The missing ingredient — the one that turns quantum weirdness into computational power — is interference.

In quantum mechanics, amplitudes are not ordinary numbers. They are complex numbers — each one carries both a magnitude and a phase, like an arrow on a clock face. When two computational paths lead to the same answer, their amplitudes add. If their phases align, they amplify each other — constructive interference. If their phases oppose, they cancel — destructive interference.

A quantum algorithm is, at its heart, a choreography of phases. The goal is to arrange things so that paths leading to wrong answers interfere destructively (cancelling out), while paths leading to the right answer interfere constructively (growing brighter). It is the same physics that creates fringes in the double-slit experiment — now harnessed to solve problems.

"Quantum computation is nothing more than a multi-particle interference experiment."

— Rolf Landauer, IBM Research

This is the deep insight. A quantum computer does not "try all answers at once" in any simple sense — that is a common misunderstanding. Rather, it arranges for the amplitudes of wrong answers to cancel each other out, leaving the right answer standing alone. It is constructive interference on the correct answer, destructive interference on everything else.

Every quantum algorithm — Shor's for factoring, Grover's for searching, quantum simulation for molecules — relies on this same geometric trick. The details vary: Shor's algorithm uses the Quantum Fourier Transform to make period-finding amplitudes constructive; Grover's uses amplitude amplification to iteratively boost the target. But the principle is always the same: orchestrate the phases so that truth survives and error cancels.

We explore these algorithms in depth in the Quantum Algorithms chapter. For now, the key lesson is that quantum speedup is not about brute-force parallelism. It is about geometry — the geometry of complex amplitudes interfering in high-dimensional space.

Section 05

Colder Than Space

우주보다 차가운 곳

The mathematics of quantum computing is beautiful. But to build an actual quantum computer, you must solve an engineering problem of staggering difficulty: keep quantum states alive long enough to compute with them. Qubits are extraordinarily fragile. A stray photon, a vibration from a passing truck, even a cosmic ray from deep space can destroy a quantum state in microseconds. The machines that protect qubits are among the most extreme engineering feats on Earth.

The dominant technology today — superconducting qubits — requires temperatures near absolute zero. Specifically, about 15 millikelvin: fifteen thousandths of a degree above the coldest temperature possible. This is roughly 200 times colder than outer space. At this temperature, electrical resistance vanishes, thermal noise drops to near zero, and quantum effects can persist long enough to be useful.

"The coldest places in the known universe aren't in the void between galaxies. They're in laboratories in Santa Barbara and Yorktown Heights."

— commonly noted in quantum hardware discussions

The refrigerator works by a process called dilution — mixing two isotopes of helium (He-3 and He-4) in a way that continuously absorbs heat. Each stage of the fridge is a different temperature, with cables, filters, and attenuators carefully designed to block heat from reaching the precious chip at the bottom.

But superconducting qubits are not the only approach. The race to build a quantum computer has produced a remarkable diversity of technologies, each with distinct strengths.

The superconducting approach uses tiny circuits made of aluminium on silicon, cooled until they superconduct. The qubit is a transmon — a resonant circuit containing a Josephson junction, a sandwich of aluminium-insulator-aluminium only nanometres thick. It behaves as a quantum two-level system with transition frequencies around 5 GHz.

Trapped ions use individual atoms (often ytterbium or calcium) held in place by electromagnetic fields in a vacuum. Laser pulses manipulate the ions' quantum states. This approach boasts the highest gate fidelities — above 99.9% — but is harder to scale to thousands of qubits.

Neutral atoms, pioneered by QuEra, trap hundreds of rubidium atoms in optical tweezers — focused laser beams. They can be reconfigured dynamically, and arrays of over 6,000 atoms have been demonstrated. This is the fastest-scaling platform in 2026.

Photonic systems use particles of light as qubits. They work at room temperature — no cryogenics needed — but measuring and entangling photons is tricky. Xanadu's Borealis has demonstrated quantum advantage for certain sampling tasks.

Finally, Microsoft's topological approach aims to encode qubits in exotic quantum states called Majorana fermions. These would be inherently resistant to noise — but they have proven extraordinarily difficult to create and verify. It remains the highest-risk, highest-reward strategy.

Section 06

The Enemy Within

내부의 적

A quantum computer's greatest enemy is not a hacker. It is a stray photon. A thermal vibration. A cosmic ray that tunnels through the cryostat and flips a qubit. Even at 15 millikelvin, errors are relentless — the best superconducting qubits today fail roughly once every thousand operations. For comparison, the error rate in a classical computer is about one in a quintillion. Closing that gap is the central challenge of quantum computing.

The solution is quantum error correction — and it is profoundly counterintuitive. You cannot simply copy a qubit to back it up, because the no-cloning theorem forbids copying unknown quantum states. Instead, you spread one logical qubit across many physical qubits, creating a redundant code that can detect and fix errors without ever reading (and thus destroying) the quantum information it protects.

"Error correction is the endgame for quantum computers."

— Julian Kelly, Director of Quantum Hardware, Google

The most promising error correction scheme is the surface code. Imagine a grid of physical qubits, like tiles on a bathroom floor. Some tiles are data qubits that carry the actual information. Between them sit syndrome qubits that continuously check their neighbours for errors — without ever reading the data itself. When a syndrome qubit detects something wrong, it raises an alarm, and a classical decoder figures out which data qubit flipped and corrects it.

The key insight: as you make the grid bigger, the logical error rate goes down — exponentially. Every time you double the grid, the error rate drops by roughly half. This is called operating "below threshold," and it was achieved for the first time by Google's Willow chip in December 2024.

Section 07

The Quantum Horizon

양자 지평선

In 1981, Richard Feynman stood before an audience at MIT and posed a question: can a classical computer efficiently simulate quantum physics? His answer was no — and his proposed solution was a computer built from quantum mechanics itself. Four decades later, Feynman's vision is becoming real.

The history of quantum computing is a story of ideas that seemed impossible, then became inevitable. Peter Shor's 1994 algorithm proved that quantum computers could factor large numbers exponentially faster than any known classical algorithm — and threatened the entire infrastructure of digital cryptography. Lov Grover's 1996 algorithm showed quadratic speedup for unstructured search. For years, these were theorems without machines. Then the machines began to arrive.

The field has entered what researchers call the fault-tolerant foundation era. The question is no longer can quantum computers work, but when will they become useful. The three stages of quantum computing hardware are:

NISQ (Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum) — the current era. Machines with 50–1,000 noisy qubits. Useful for certain benchmarks and proof-of-concept experiments, but too error-prone for practical algorithms.

Early fault-tolerant — expected within 3–5 years. Machines with thousands of physical qubits implementing dozens of logical qubits with error rates low enough for short algorithms. First real-world applications in chemistry and materials simulation.

Large-scale fault-tolerant — the end goal, perhaps by the mid-2030s. Millions of physical qubits, thousands of logical qubits, error rates below one in a trillion. This is where quantum computers will simulate molecular interactions that are intractable on any classical supercomputer, revolutionise drug discovery, crack current encryption, and optimise systems of enormous complexity.

The catch

The surprise: quantum computers are not universally faster. For sorting, classical algorithms are already essentially optimal. For training neural networks, there is no known exponential quantum speedup. Quantum advantage is surgical — it strikes where the problem's structure allows interference to work its magic. Factoring, simulation of quantum systems, and certain optimisation problems are the sweet spots.

"Nature isn't classical, dammit, and if you want to make a simulation of nature, you'd better make it quantum mechanical, and by golly it's a wonderful problem, because it doesn't look so easy."

— Richard Feynman, 1981

The coin that lands on both sides is no longer just a thought experiment. It is a chip in a cryostat, an atom in a laser trap, a photon in a waveguide. Nature computes with interference and entanglement — and we are learning to speak its language.
