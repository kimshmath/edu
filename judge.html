02 — CASE #2: PREDICTING HEALTH

Healthcare Algorithm Affecting 200 Million

In 2019, Obermeyer (Berkeley) & Mullainathan (Chicago) found severe racial bias in a healthcare algorithm affecting ~200M US patients.Obermeyer et al. Science, 2019

Black patients enrolled
17.7%
Variable used
Healthcare costs

Told to predict "health," it actually predicted "spending." Less spending = healthier, it assumed.

Due to structural inequality, equally sick Black patients spend less on healthcare. The algorithm misjudged them as "healthier."

After using actual health indicators, Black enrollment rose from 17.7% to 46.5%. Bias reduced by 84%.Obermeyer et al. 2019

The proxy trap. Even without using race directly, variables correlated with race produce bias. Obermeyer: "Convenient proxies can be an important source of algorithmic bias."Obermeyer et al. 2019

03 — THIS IS NOT A BUG

The Impossibility Theorem

Researchers at Cornell, Stanford, Harvard, CMU independently proved this is not fixable — it is a mathematical limit.Kleinberg, Mullainathan & Raghavan, 2016; Chouldechova, 2017

Three fairness criteria — one must go

When base rates differ between groups, no algorithm can satisfy all three simultaneously.

① Calibration

Score X means the same reoffending probability for all groups

= Northpointe's "fair"

② FP Balance

False high-risk rate is equal across groups for non-reoffenders

= ProPublica's "fair"

③ FN Balance

False low-risk rate is equal across groups for reoffenders

= "Not missing dangerous people"

Click a card to sacrifice one criterion

Kleinberg, Mullainathan & Raghavan (2016): "As long as base rates differ, no method can satisfy these three fairness conditions simultaneously."

Not a bug — a mathematical theorem. No programmer can overcome it. Three reasonable fairness definitions are incompatible, meaning we must choose what "fair" means.

05 — THE DEEPER QUESTION

Whose Fairness?

The real lesson: algorithmic fairness is not a technical but a values problem.

Northpointe's View

"If scores mean the same for all groups, it's fair."

Calibration first. Score 7 = 70% for everyone.

ProPublica's View

"If innocents are misclassified at different rates, it's unfair."

FPR balance first. Harm to innocents must be equal.

Both reasonable. But both cannot be satisfied. Different base rates make this mathematically impossible.

The real question:

When an algorithm is inevitably unfair —
who bears the cost of that unfairness?

Not math's domain — politics and morality. The illusion of "objectivity" is dangerous because it makes this choice invisibly.

CLOSING

A machine judges.
Quickly, consistently, at scale.

But what "fair judgment" means —
a machine cannot decide.

That three reasonable fairness definitions

is not a limitation of algorithms.

The danger of delegating judgment
is not that machines are biased.

It is that machines hide the fact
that bias in judgment is inevitable.
